Markets — What You’re Selling

Feed barley led this week's grain rally, up £4.90/t to £153.30/t ex-farm (AHDB), a 3.3% week-on-week gain driven by renewed livestock demand and tighter Northern European stocks. Feed wheat followed with a £3.10/t lift to £168.50/t, supported by firmer export premiums and reduced Black Sea availability. Milling wheat held steady at £167.70/t, slipping just 20p as millers remain adequately covered through Q2. The feed barley surge narrows the discount to wheat to £15.20/t — watch May-June contracts as compound buyers may switch back to wheat if the gap tightens further. Forward curves remain inverted; any rally extension favours spot sales over deferred positions.

Costs — What You’re Paying

Fertiliser, feed and fuel

Fertiliser prices climbed sharply in February (AHDB). Granular urea jumped £28/t to £455/t, while DAP rose £46/t to £719/t — both significant month-on-month moves. UK-produced AN increased £9.50/t to £402/t. With spring top-dressing underway, waiting on further N purchases looks risky given current trajectory. Feed ingredients (AHDB) edged up week-on-week to 13 March: soyameal hi-pro rose £5/t to £324/t, Brazilian 48% to £337/t, and pelleted wheat feed £1/t to £152/t. Regional variation applies. Fuel prices not provided.

From the Soil

For two centuries, Norfolk turkeys were the Christmas bird for London — not sent by train or lorry, but walked. Every autumn, huge flocks of turkeys, geese, and cattle set out from the farms of Norfolk on the drove roads south, a journey of two to three weeks. Turkeys were fitted with leather boots to protect their feet on the flint roads. Drovers camped at established inns along the route. The trade collapsed with the arrival of railways in the 1840s, which could move a live turkey from Norwich to London in hours. The Norfolk Bronze turkey is now a rare breed, kept by small flocks of dedicated breeders.
