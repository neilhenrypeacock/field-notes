I

Before the Field Had a Name

Six thousand years ago, the land beneath your feet was not yet fields. It was wildwood — great oak and lime stretching from the chalk rises of the Chilterns down to the slow fingers of the Wash. Aurochs moved through glades. Beavers dammed the young rivers. And then, gradually, people began to clear.

The first farmers arrived in East Anglia around 4000 BC, Neolithic settlers who had crossed from the continent with cattle, seed corn, and the knowledge of what cultivation could do. They felled the trees with stone axes. They broke the dark, heavy soil and buried their grain. And they stayed — because what they found here was extraordinary.

The soils of East Anglia are among the finest on earth. Deep, free-draining, stone-light chalks in the west; the magnificent loams of mid-Suffolk and Norfolk; the ancient peats of the Fens so dark they seem to absorb the sky. Those first people would have sensed it immediately.

This is a place that has always wanted to grow things.

II

The Romans and the Iceni

By the time the Romans arrived in 43 AD, East Anglia was already a significant agricultural region. The Iceni — the tribe whose queen Boudica would make one of the most dramatic rebellions in British history — farmed this land with Celtic field systems, raised horses and cattle, and grew emmer wheat and spelt along the river valleys. The Roman invaders found a people who were already, unmistakably, farmers.

The Romans brought heavier iron ploughs that could turn the deeper clay soils, new crop varieties, and crucially, the first drainage works in the Fens — the long, slow argument with water that would define Fenland farming for the next two thousand years.

When the Romans withdrew, much of what they had built fell back to marsh and scrub. But the fields remained. The knowledge remained. And the farmers remained — a little less organised, perhaps, but still here.

III

The Wool Kingdom

Medieval East Anglia was, for several centuries, the wealthiest region in England. And that wealth came from sheep.

The great flocks of Norfolk and Suffolk were not kept merely for mutton. Their wool — fine, abundant, prized — fed the looms of Flemish weavers and built the soaring churches that still stand in every village: Lavenham, Long Melford, Blythburgh, Salle. Wool churches, they are called. The fleece paid for the tower; the tithes of barley paid for the nave.

But behind the wool was always the grass, and behind the grass was always the arable. The famous Norfolk four-course rotation — turnips, barley, clover, wheat — may not have been formalised until the eighteenth century, but its logic was understood long before that. Rest the land, feed the sheep, fold the sheep on the barley, watch the barley yield double.

The farmers of medieval East Anglia were not romantic figures scratching at the earth. They were practical, systematic people who understood their soils with extraordinary intimacy. They had to. A failed harvest was not a financial problem — it was a death sentence for the village.

IV

The Revolution That Began Here

In 1730, a Norfolk landowner named Viscount Townshend retired from politics, went back to his estate at Raynham, and started growing turnips. History found this amusing enough to call him "Turnip Townshend." But what he and his neighbours — Coke of Holkham, the Bakewell-influenced breeders, the experimenting tenant farmers of the clay lands — were doing was extraordinary.

They were inventing modern agriculture.

The Agricultural Revolution happened here, in this corner of England, in the fields you can still see from the A11 and the B1077. The four-course rotation, selective livestock breeding, under-drainage, early mechanisation — all of it pioneered in East Anglia. Yields doubled. Then they doubled again. The population of England grew, and it was the farms of Norfolk and Suffolk that fed them.

Holkham Hall still runs a farming operation. The estate villages — built by Coke to house his improved tenants — are still there. The land, drained and shaped by those Georgian improvers, still produces barley and wheat on the same rotations they refined two centuries ago.

V

Draining the Deep Dark Fens

To understand East Anglian farming is to understand the Fens. And to understand the Fens, you have to understand that this landscape — the flattest, most open farmland in Britain — did not always exist. Most of it was built.

For centuries, the Fens were a vast inland sea of reed and sedge, mist and wildfowl. Ely was literally an island. In the 1630s, the fourth Earl of Bedford hired the Dutch engineer Cornelius Vermuyden and set him to drain one hundred thousand acres. The result provoked riots and sabotage from the Fen commoners who saw their world being drained away. But the drainage continued. Windmills gave way to steam pumps; steam pumps gave way to diesel and electric.

The peat, exposed to the air, began to shrink and oxidise. The Fens sank, slowly, below sea level. Today, some fields sit four, five, even six metres below the high tide mark of the Wash.

One of the most productive agricultural landscapes on earth — dark, friable, astonishingly fertile peat soils. But they are finite. Farming the Fens has always been a negotiation with impermanence.

VI

The Machines Arrive

On a morning in August 1879, a gang of labourers at a farm near Bury St Edmunds turned up to find a steam threshing engine had arrived overnight. Within a decade, the casual army of harvest workers — the gangs who had moved around East Anglia every summer for centuries, following the ripening from south to north — would be mostly gone.

Mechanisation came hard and fast to East Anglia because East Anglia's farms were large enough to justify the machines. Broad, flat, open acres — exactly what a threshing machine, and later a tractor, needed.

The first tractors arrived around 1910. By the 1940s, with wartime food production at crisis levels, the government was ploughing up old meadows across Suffolk and Norfolk at furious speed.

The Women's Land Army worked these fields while the men were in France. East Anglia fed a nation under siege.

VII

The Prairie Years and What Came After

The decades after 1945 brought the greatest transformation East Anglia had seen since the Fenland drains. Subsidised by the state and driven by the logic of efficiency, farms merged and expanded. Hedgerows came out — miles and miles of them, the ancient boundaries of medieval strips and Tudor enclosures, ripped from the clay with mechanical grubbers and burned. From the air, East Anglia began to look like the American Midwest.

Skylark numbers collapsed. Grey partridges vanished from parishes that had always held them. The great arable prairies were producing more grain than ever before — and losing something harder to measure.

But the story didn't stop there. It never does. By the 1990s and 2000s, farmers themselves were leading the conversation about what had been lost and what might be restored. Conservation headlands. Beetle banks. Cover crops. Regenerative rotations. The great farms of East Anglia — often the very same estates that had stripped the hedges — began replanting them. Not out of sentiment, but because the science caught up with the instinct: the land works better when it isn't just a factory.

VIII

This Land, This Week

The farm you work today stands in a line that runs unbroken from those first Neolithic clearances. The field names on your tenancy or your title deeds — the Eleven Acres, the Burnt Ground, the Horseshoe piece, the Long Fen — are often older than the English language as we speak it. Saxon, Norse, Norman-French, all layered into the names of the places where you park your tractor.

You are farming soils that have been farmed, understood, and cared for by hundreds of generations of people before you. People who watched the same skies and worried about the same frosts. People who read the tilt of the land and the colour of the soil the way you read a yield map. People who made decisions under uncertainty — weather, prices, politics — exactly as you do now.

The pressures have changed. The tools have changed, spectacularly. But the essential transaction — a person, a piece of land, and the work of making it produce — that has not changed at all.

Field Notes exists because that transaction deserves to be supported with the best possible information, every single week. Not noise. Not content. Just the numbers, the weather, the schemes, and the stories that actually matter.
